Why your resume keeps getting filtered

What ATS systems actually look for

Applicant Tracking Systems aren't reading your resume the way a human does. They're scanning for specific keywords, phrases, and formatting patterns that match what's in the job description. A resume that doesn't surface those signals gets scored poorly — regardless of how qualified the person is.

The problem isn't your experience. It's that your resume isn't speaking the same language as the job posting. The Fit Scanner analyzes both and shows you exactly where the gaps are.

The difference between a generic resume and a targeted one

A generic resume summarizes your career. A targeted resume is a direct response to a specific job description — it uses the employer's language, surfaces the skills they're prioritizing, and frames your experience in terms of what they're solving for.

The Fit Scanner shows you what a targeted resume looks like for each specific role you're applying to. Once you see your keyword gaps and the suggested edits, you can make changes in minutes — not hours.

Does it work for all industries?

Yes. The Fit Scanner works for any role in any industry — from technology and finance to healthcare, education, and non-profit. The underlying logic is the same: how well does this resume's language align with this posting's requirements?

Whether you're applying for an entry-level position or an executive role, the analysis gives you the same level of specificity. The keywords and phrasing differ by industry, but the approach is universal.

How many times should I run it?

Once per application. The scan is specific to the job posting you provide — results for a marketing manager role will look completely different from results for a sales manager role, even with the same resume. Run it for each new application to get the most accurate picture.

If you make changes based on the recommendations and want to verify your score improved, run it a second time. The free tier gives you 3 runs per day.

Is this the same as keyword stuffing?

No. Keyword stuffing — cramming a resume with every possible keyword regardless of context — actually hurts you. It reads as inauthentic to both software and humans, and experienced recruiters flag it immediately.

The Fit Scanner identifies keywords that should appear naturally in your resume given your actual experience. The edits it suggests are about framing your real work in the language the employer uses — not fabricating qualifications you don't have.
